LACK OF HEALTH SERVICES

Compared with people living in urban areas,

Australians living in rural and remote areas tend to have shorter lives, higher levels of illness and harm,

and fewer opportunities to access and use health services.

Poor health outcomes in rural and remote areas

may be due to a range of factors,

including education and employment opportunities, income

and access to health services.

  • Compared with major cities, suicide rates in remote areas are 1.7 times higher.
  • 5.4 times the death rate from land transport accidents in remote areas compared to major cities.

Maria Berry, who lives near Wodonga,

on the border of Victoria and New South Wales,

says the demand already exceeds the choices.

“In our area, there is one Geriatrician with a long waiting list for people wanting assessments

and specialist advice prior to entering aged care” 

Additional costs

There are extra costs for time and money

associated with going to these appointments.

Ms. Berry pointed out that

public transport options are usually rare, taxis are expensive,

carers may not be available for a day trip.

Some local services are not feasible in rural areas,

these services are being closed

due to staffing or funding issues.

Uncertainty

Dr. Judith Anderson,

senior lecturer at the Charles Sturt University School of Nursing,

Midwifery and Tertiary Health,

also pointed out the uncertainty of rural services.

“The former government told the provider that 16 beds were sustainable and now they are told it is not sustainable,” she said.

“Every time the government changes, it will affect the community."
